> **Analysis Focus:** Every security team claims to do analysis. Very few can explain what that word means, how a defensible judgment differs from an educated guess, or why smart analysts produce confident wrong answers. This post is about the discipline itself.

**TL;DR:** Analysis is not summarizing reports or enriching indicators. It is the production of a defensible judgment under incomplete information: an assessment with explicit confidence, stated assumptions, and acknowledged gaps. Your brain reaches conclusions through pattern matching machinery that is fast, useful, and systematically biased, and the deception operations of the last decade were built to exploit exactly that. The fix is not brilliance. It is structure: externalized reasoning, competing hypotheses, and a process that catches bias before your consumers pay for it.

## Introduction

Ask a threat intelligence team what they produce and you will hear about reports, feeds, briefings, and dashboards. Ask them what analysis is, the activity that supposedly produced all of that, and the answers get vague. Reading vendor reporting is not analysis. Pivoting on infrastructure is not analysis. Tagging indicators with ATT&CK techniques is not analysis. All of those are inputs.

The traditional intelligence community spent seventy years professionalizing what happens between collection and conclusion, largely because getting it wrong cost lives and occasionally started wars. <Term concept="cti">Cyber threat intelligence</Term> inherited the vocabulary of that field but frequently skipped the tradecraft. This post covers the part that got skipped: what analysis actually is, how the human brain performs it, the specific ways it fails, and the structured techniques that keep those failures out of your finished products.

## What Analysis Actually Is

The word comes from Greek roots meaning to loosen up or unpick. Analysis is the act of taking something apart to understand how it works: an intrusion decomposed into phases, a campaign decomposed into incidents, a binary decomposed into capabilities. That is the half everyone knows.

The half that gets forgotten is synthesis. Intelligence work is not finished when the pieces are on the table. The analyst recombines them with history, with telemetry from other victims, with knowledge of adversary economics and geopolitics, and produces something that was not present in any single input: an assessment. Taking apart an intrusion tells you what happened on your network. Synthesis tells you what it means and what is likely to happen next. An analyst who only decomposes is a very thorough clerk.

It helps to be precise about the raw material, because data, information, and intelligence are not synonyms. Walk one example up the ladder. The fact that `hr-payroll-view[.]com` resolved to 198.51.100.7 yesterday is data: a value with no meaning attached. The finding that this domain serves a credential phishing kit also present on six sibling domains sharing one TLS certificate is information: data organized to answer a factual question. The finding that three of your finance employees received the lure this week is also information. The statement that this is broad opportunistic credential harvesting rather than targeting of your sector, held with medium confidence because the kit's victim list spans more than forty unrelated industries, is intelligence. Only the last statement is a judgment. Only the last statement tells a decision maker what to do about MFA enforcement versus a full targeted-threat response.

Notice what the judgment does not say. It does not claim certainty, because you never have all the collection. Intelligence customers want yes or no. What the discipline can honestly deliver is an assessment with a confidence level and a record of what would change it.

One more inheritance from the traditional world matters here. Classic intelligence organized itself around collection disciplines: <Term concept="humint">HUMINT</Term> from human sources, <Term concept="sigint">SIGINT</Term> from intercepted signals, <Term concept="osint">OSINT</Term> from public material, and so on, with dedicated agencies cultivating deliberate specialization in each. CTI does not get that luxury. Nobody is running agents inside a ransomware crew for you. A CTI analyst is an <Term concept="all-source">all-source</Term> analyst by necessity, fusing malware analysis, infrastructure data, victimology, and open reporting. That is a strength, but it carries a warning: every analyst falls in love with one source. The reverse engineer trusts binaries, the network analyst trusts <Term concept="passive-dns">passive DNS</Term>, and each quietly overweights what their favorite source can see. Treat every source as one data point in an overall assessment, not as the assessment.

## The People Who Professionalized It

Two names anchor everything in this post, and both are worth knowing because their work is free and directly applicable.

Sherman Kent was a Yale history professor who joined the OSS during World War II and spent seventeen Cold War years at the CIA, where he chaired the Board of National Estimates. He is routinely called the father of intelligence analysis, mostly because he insisted, in an era of gut-feel operators, that analysis was a profession with standards, methods, and accountability, and wrote *Strategic Intelligence for American World Policy* to prove it. The principles later distilled from his work as Kent's analytic doctrine read like they were written for a modern security team:

| Kent's principle | What it means for your team |
|---|---|
| Focus on policymaker concerns | Serve the decision your CISO, IR lead, or SOC actually faces. "Interesting" is not a standard; useful to a decision is. |
| Avoid a personal policy agenda | Assess what the evidence supports, not what justifies the tooling budget you want. |
| Intellectual rigor | Reasoning must survive being written down and examined. |
| Conscious effort to avoid analytic biases | Bias management is an explicit work item, not an aspiration. |
| Willingness to consider other judgments | A challenge to your assessment is quality control, not an insult. |
| Systematic use of outside experts | Ten threat intel analysts who all came from the same background will miss the same things. Ask the DBA, the finance lead, the OT engineer. |
| Collective responsibility for judgment | The team owns the assessment that ships, not the individual who drafted it. |
| Effective communication | Skip the jargon. A judgment nobody understands changed no decisions. |
| Candid admission of mistakes | Publish corrections with the same energy as the original call. Calibration only improves if errors are acknowledged. |

Richards Heuer spent forty-five years at the CIA and distilled what he saw into *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis*, a book the agency publishes free and which remains the single most useful text a CTI analyst can read. Heuer's central claim is quietly radical: the biggest analytic failures do not come from missing data. They come from how analysts think. He described the analyst's function as transcending the limits of incomplete information through the exercise of analytic judgment, and then spent a career studying why that judgment goes wrong and how to structure around it. His Analysis of Competing Hypotheses, which we will run end to end below, is the most practical output of that career.

## Analytic Judgment: The Actual Product

An <Term concept="analytic-judgment">analytic judgment</Term> is what an intelligence function actually produces. Not indicators, not reports as a format, but a specific composite: an assessment, the confidence behind it, the evidence and assumptions it rests on, and the gaps that remain. A judgment about the past ("the intrusion's objective was pre-positioning, not theft") and a judgment about the future ("blocking this <Term concept="c2">C2</Term> channel will likely trigger re-entry within a week") are both legitimate products. A list of facts with no assessment attached is not.

Hold the judgment to the same standard you would hold a forensic finding: defensible, repeatable, and understandable by someone who was not in the room. Another analyst should be able to walk your reasoning from evidence to conclusion and find where they disagree. If your judgment cannot survive that walk, it is an opinion wearing a confidence level.

Confidence itself needs a rubric, or the words high and low mean whatever each analyst feels that day. The one I use is deliberately short. High confidence requires multiple independent sources plus corroborating telemetry, with recent validation. Medium means good supporting evidence from fewer sources or partial telemetry linkage, with identified gaps outstanding. Low means single-source reporting, uncorroborated signals, or a judgment resting on significant untested assumptions. Whatever rubric you adopt, write the top two supporting facts and the one gap that would most reduce confidence directly into the product, so the consumer can calibrate rather than trust.

Heuer also gave us a useful distinction about how judgments get made in the first place. Some problems are data-driven: the dataset is good, the model is established, and the analysis is essentially the disciplined application of one to the other. Other problems are conceptually driven: the variables are undefined, nobody agrees which ones matter, and the analyst's <Term concept="mental-model">mental model</Term> is doing most of the work.

| Dimension | Data-driven analysis | Conceptually driven analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Works when | Dataset and model are both solid | Unknowns dominate and no settled model exists |
| CTI examples | Detection coverage metrics, infrastructure clustering, campaign volume trends | Attribution, adversary intent, "what will they do next" |
| Accuracy driven by | Data quality and model fit | The analyst's mental models and feedback over time |
| Replication | Another analyst gets the same answer | Analysts can reasonably disagree |
| Failure mode | Garbage in, confident garbage out | Unexamined assumptions masquerading as findings |

Most of the CTI questions that matter are conceptually driven, which is exactly where "it depends" lives. That answer is legitimate on one condition: it must be followed by what it depends on. "It depends on whether the actor is financially motivated, which evidence X would settle" is analysis. "It depends" followed by silence is a shrug.

## How Your Brain Analyzes, and How It Betrays You

Heuer opens his book with perception, because analysis happens inside a perceptual system that is not a passive camera. You do not see what is there. You see what your expectations, experience, and current hypothesis prepare you to see. The sentence contains six. Most readers find three. The three that disappear all sit inside the word OF, and they disappear for a reason worth understanding: a fluent reader never processes OF letter by letter. It is recognized as a whole, heard internally as "ov" rather than spelled with an F, and skipped as a low-value function word. The letters are right there on the page, and your perceptual system filtered them out before the conscious part of you ever got a vote. If perception edits a single sentence during an exercise where you were explicitly told what to look for, consider what it does to a hundred pages of intrusion data read under time pressure with a hypothesis already in mind.

The experimental version of this is the famous invisible gorilla study by Simons and Chabris. Subjects watching a basketball video and counting passes were so occupied by the counting task that roughly half failed to notice a person in a gorilla suit walk through the middle of the frame, stop, and beat its chest. The analysts' translation: telling someone precisely what to look for is also telling them what they will fail to see. A hunt team tasked narrowly on one actor's tradecraft will walk straight past the second actor in the environment.

Under the perception layer sits the machinery that does the actual matching. Working memory is the small, expensive workspace where inputs get compared and judged, and it overflows quickly, which is why analysts reach for models and buckets at all. Long-term memory supplies the patterns, and it does so in three recognizable modes. Template matching compares the current situation against stored past experiences. Prototype matching compares it against an averaged composite of many experiences, which is what lets you recognize something you have never seen an exact copy of. Top-down matching fills gaps in incomplete information using what you already know, which is why context helps and also why optical illusions work.

All three run constantly during triage, and none of them are verification. When an analyst glances at beacon timing and says "that looks like <Term concept="cobalt-strike">Cobalt Strike</Term>," that is prototype matching: fast, frequently right, and worth exactly nothing as evidence until checked, because plenty of frameworks imitate that traffic profile and plenty of red teams run the real thing legitimately.

The cleanest frame for managing all this is Kahneman's dual process model. System 1 is intuitive thinking: fast, automatic, built on the pattern machinery above, and usually right in familiar territory. System 2 is analytic thinking: slow, deliberate, effortful, and the only mode in which structured reasoning happens.

| | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Character | Fast, intuitive, automatic | Slow, methodical, conscious |
| Runs on | Mental models and accumulated experience | Deliberate reasoning and explicit process |
| SOC example | Phishing ticket number 47 of the day, closed in ninety seconds | An attribution assessment that will reach the CISO |
| Cost | Cheap, always on | Expensive, easily exhausted |
| Failure mode | Confident misclassification of the one ticket that was different | Skipped entirely because there was no time |

The skill is not suppressing System 1, which is impossible and would make you useless in a fast-moving incident. The skill is knowing which decisions are allowed to stay in System 1 and which must be forced into System 2. Experienced analysts deliberately push routine calls into intuition precisely to reserve their limited System 2 budget for the judgments with consequences. The failure pattern worth fearing is the inverse: a consequential judgment, an attribution, a "this is not targeted" call, an all-clear, made in System 1 because it pattern-matched something familiar.

## Mental Models: Putting Data Into Buckets

Mental models earned a mention above as the fuel of System 1, and they deserve their own section because CTI has formalized several of them into shared frameworks. The <Term concept="kill-chain">kill chain</Term> and the <Term concept="diamond-model">Diamond Model</Term> are, at bottom, agreements about buckets: named containers that structure information so working memory does not have to hold everything at once.

Sorting data into buckets does two things that unsorted notes cannot. It exposes gaps, because an empty bucket is a visible, nagging question ("we have the delivery and the C2, why do we have nothing on lateral movement?"). And it enables pattern recognition across cases, because two intrusions bucketed the same way can be compared phase by phase, which is how campaigns get identified out of what first looked like unrelated incidents.

The caution is that every model carries axioms, and the model only works where its axioms hold. The Diamond Model paper is admirably explicit about its own. The kill chain quietly assumes an external adversary progressing inward through phases, which is why it fits an insider case so badly. A model applied outside its assumptions does not fail loudly. It keeps producing tidy, structured, wrong pictures. State the axioms and you know when to put the model down.

## Where Analysis Breaks: Bias

Everyone has biases. They cannot be removed, because they are a side effect of the same simplification machinery that lets you function at all. The realistic goal is a process that catches bias, not an analyst who lacks it.

The case study that should be taught in every CTI onboarding is Olympic Destroyer. During the opening ceremony of the Pyeongchang Winter Olympics in February 2018, a wiper took down the Games' IT infrastructure: Wi-Fi, ticketing, the official website. Analysts worldwide dove into the sample, and the sample rewarded them. Its Rich header, a compiler artifact many analysts treat as a reliable fingerprint, matched a wiper previously used by the North Korean Lazarus group, and North Korea attributions promptly appeared in reporting. Then researchers at Kaspersky's GReAT examined the header more closely and found it was forged. The metadata did not match the code it sat on. Someone had deliberately planted Lazarus's fingerprint inside the malware, anticipating precisely which artifact analysts would trust and how fast they would run with it. The operation was later attributed to Russia's GRU: the October 2020 US indictment of six Sandworm officers lists the Olympic attack among their operations.

Read as a bias lesson, the sequence is uncomfortable. The header felt diagnostic because it usually is, so it anchored the analysis. Everything found afterward was read in light of the North Korea hypothesis, which is <Term concept="confirmation-bias">confirmation bias</Term> operating exactly as designed. The geopolitical context made the hypothesis satisfying. And the adversary had engineered the entire experience, which is the modern reality: a competent <Term concept="threat-actor">threat actor</Term> knows your analytic shortcuts and can afford to feed them. A <Term concept="false-flag">false flag</Term> is an exploit against methodology, and the vulnerable component is the unexamined judgment.

The pattern was not new. In April 2015, the French broadcaster TV5Monde was taken off the air across a dozen channels while its hijacked social accounts displayed the banner of the CyberCaliphate, an apparently ISIS-aligned group. The self-attribution anchored early coverage, as self-attribution reliably does. The subsequent investigation pointed instead to APT28, the Russian military intelligence group, using the jihadist persona as cover. The general rule: a claim of responsibility is not evidence about who did it. It is evidence about who someone wants you to think did it, and it should enter your matrix priced accordingly.

Two quieter biases round out the set. <Term concept="mirror-imaging">Mirror imaging</Term> is assuming the adversary reasons like you: that they will value what you would value, avoid what you would avoid, and take the path that looks optimal from inside your head. Adversaries run on different economics, doctrine, and constraints, and the canonical demonstration comes from the traditional world: the Iraq WMD estimate, dissected by the Silberman-Robb commission as a case of assumptions so entrenched that contradicting evidence was explained away. The commission's findings are a free education in how a professional apparatus, staffed with smart people, collectively reasons its way to a confident wrong answer.

The last one is structural rather than psychological: field-of-view bias. Your picture of the threat landscape is a function of your collection, and so is every vendor's. A firm whose customer base sits in Western finance sees the actors who target Western finance. An OT-focused vendor sees ICS-shaped threats. A vendor with heavy Middle East telemetry writes reports about actors your US-centric feeds barely mention. None of them are lying. All of them are describing the part of the ocean their nets reach, and only a fraction of the world's active intrusion sets operates in any single region or sector. Every time you ingest third-party reporting without asking what that vendor can and cannot see, you import their field of view as if it were the whole picture. The same applies to your own telemetry, which is why the team-level control for all of this is diversity in the honest, practical sense: analysts with different source loyalties, backgrounds, and mental models, empowered to challenge each other's judgments before publication. Ten reverse engineers produce malware-shaped intelligence. That is not a hiring slogan; it is collection coverage for your analytic process.

## Structured Analytic Techniques

<Term concept="structured-analytic-techniques">Structured analytic techniques</Term> are the intelligence community's accumulated answer to everything above. Each one externalizes a piece of reasoning that would otherwise happen invisibly inside one analyst's head, and puts it on a surface where it can be examined, challenged, and reproduced. Heuer and Pherson's reference catalogs dozens of techniques organized into six families, which are worth knowing as a map even if you only ever use a handful.

| Family | Question it serves | Representative techniques |
|---|---|---|
| Getting organized | What do we have? | Sorting, chronologies, timelines, checklists |
| Exploration | What are we not thinking of? | Structured brainstorming, mind maps, starbursting |
| Diagnostic | Which explanation fits? | ACH, key assumptions check, diagnostic reasoning |
| Reframing | What if we are wrong? | Devil's advocacy, red team analysis, what-if analysis |
| Foresight | What could happen next? | Scenario analysis, indicators of change |
| Decision support | What should we do? | Force field analysis, decision matrices |

Two moves recur through nearly all of them: decomposition, breaking the problem into parts small enough to consider honestly, and visualization, laying the parts out so relationships and gaps become visible. If you remember nothing else about SATs, remember that reasoning left inside a skull cannot be quality-controlled.

## ACH, End to End

<Term concept="ach">Analysis of Competing Hypotheses</Term> is the diagnostic technique that most directly attacks confirmation bias, and it deserves a full walkthrough because the mechanics are where the value lives.

The scenario. Your organization's public web server is defaced. The page carries the banner of a previously unknown collective and ideological messaging, and it lands within days of a geopolitical flashpoint involving your country. The pressure to declare this a politically motivated attack arrives within the hour, and it arrives from above.

Ordinary analysis starts with the leading explanation and collects support for it, which after the last section should worry you. ACH inverts the procedure. First, enumerate the full set of plausible hypotheses, including the uncomfortable ones: a genuine hacktivist collective, a state actor operating behind a hacktivist front, an opportunistic defacer mass-exploiting a public vulnerability, an insider disguising a grievance as an intrusion. Second, list the evidence, all of it, not just the memorable pieces. Third, score each piece of evidence against every hypothesis: consistent, inconsistent, or neutral. Fourth, and this is the move everyone gets wrong, judge hypotheses by their inconsistent evidence, not their consistent evidence.

The reason is <Term concept="diagnosticity">diagnosticity</Term>. Consistent evidence is cheap. The defacement's claim of responsibility is consistent with the hacktivist hypothesis, and with the false flag hypothesis, and with the opportunist who added a persona for flair, and even with the insider. Evidence that fits everything distinguishes nothing, no matter how vivid it feels. Inconsistent evidence is what actually eliminates hypotheses, so the assessment goes to the hypothesis with the least evidence against it, which is a very different thing from the hypothesis with the most evidence for it.

Run with the suggested scoring, the matrix does something instructive. The politically exciting hypotheses survive on consistent evidence and die on inconsistent evidence: a targeted state operation fits the timing beautifully and then collides with forty unrelated victims and an absence of reconnaissance. The claim of responsibility, the row that anchored the entire early conversation, turns out to be scored identically across the board, meaning it carries zero diagnostic weight. And the hypothesis that survives is the boring one, the opportunist riding a three-week-old public exploit, which no one in the first hour wanted to brief because it makes a worse story. ACH did mechanically what the Olympic Destroyer responders needed: it forced the forged fingerprint to compete against alternatives instead of being confirmed in isolation.

The final ACH step is a sensitivity check, and it belongs in the shipped product. Ask which single piece of evidence, if it collapsed, would flip the judgment. Here it is the co-victim telemetry: if those forty defacements turned out to be different actors reusing a public template rather than one actor's sweep, the opportunist hypothesis loses its strongest support and the false flag regains ground. That dependency gets written into the assessment explicitly, so the consumer knows exactly what future fact would trigger a revision. A judgment that names its own kill switch is a judgment a decision maker can trust.

## The Analyst's Minimum Toolkit

After all of that, the tooling section is deliberately anticlimactic. Structured analysis needs a way to capture ideas quickly without interrupting thought, which a mind map, a whiteboard, or paper handles fine. It needs a way to sort and pivot data until patterns surface, and a spreadsheet remains the most underrated tool in threat intelligence for exactly this. And it needs a written judgment format, so that every product leaving the team carries its confidence, assumptions, and gaps rather than just its conclusion. That is the entire hardware requirement. Teams routinely spend six figures on platforms while shipping judgments that would not survive Step 4 of a worksheet that fits on one page. The bar for tooling is low. The bar for discipline is the whole game.

## Hands-On Exercise

Take one recent public attribution claim: a vendor blog post or a government advisory naming an actor. Write down the source's primary hypothesis, then force yourself to write two alternatives that could also explain the reported evidence. List the evidence for and against each of the three, and run it through a mini ACH, scoring by refutation rather than support. Assign your own confidence level using the rubric from this post, and name the single assumption that, if wrong, flips the call.

Then compare your result against the source's language. Did they publish a judgment, with confidence, alternatives, and gaps? Or did they publish a hypothesis dressed as a conclusion? You will find both in the wild, and learning to tell them apart in other people's work is the fastest way to stop shipping the second kind yourself.

## Conclusion

Analysis is a discipline, not a talent. The machinery in your head that makes you fast, the pattern matching, the mental models, the intuition built from a thousand triaged incidents, is the same machinery that hid half the letters in a one-sentence counting test and handed half the industry a forged North Korean fingerprint. None of that is fixable, and the analysts who believe they are personally unbiased are the ones adversaries design for.

What separates an assessment from a guess is not the analyst's brilliance. It is whether the reasoning was written down, whether alternatives genuinely competed, whether the evidence was scored for what it could refute rather than what it could support, and whether the finished judgment carries its confidence, its assumptions, and the name of the fact that would kill it. That is a process, it fits on a page, and it is the difference between intelligence work and confident storytelling.
