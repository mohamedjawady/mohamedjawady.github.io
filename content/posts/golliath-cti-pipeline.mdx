> **Authorized research only.** Everything described here was conducted in a controlled environment against data I had lawful authorization to analyze. Golliath does not facilitate unauthorized access, credential use, or victim contact of any kind.

Every day, hundreds of Telegram channels publish stealer logs, archives packed with credentials, cookies, and system fingerprints from compromised machines. Most <Term concept="Threat Intelligence" /> teams consume this data passively, via commercial feeds, by the time the intelligence is already stale. I wanted to get closer to the source: build something that scraped channels in real time, parsed the archive formats properly, and gave me a structured, searchable view of what was being shared.

That project became **Golliath**, a six-subsystem platform written in Go and Python, backed by Kafka, OpenSearch, Neo4j, MinIO, and PostgreSQL, with a Next.js frontend for exploration. This post is a deep dive into how it works, why it's built the way it is, and what the numbers look like after running it against a real-world corpus.

---

## 1. Why Telegram? Why Now?

Telegram has become the dominant distribution channel for <Term concept="Initial Access Broker" /> activity and stealer-log marketplaces. The reasons are structural: channels support large file attachments (up to 4 GB), messages are persistent and indexed by Telegram's own servers, and invite-link-based access creates a discoverable social graph of threat actor infrastructure.

Unlike paste sites or dark-web forums, Telegram does not require Tor and responds well to programmatic access via MTProto. The channel topology itself is intelligence: who forwards whom, which channels share an admin, how a new log pack propagates across the ecosystem within hours of publication.

The challenge is volume. A single active channel might post dozens of archives per day, and a monitoring operation watching 50+ channels accumulates data faster than any manual triage workflow can handle. The only way to turn that volume into signal is automation, and automation requires understanding the archive formats.

## 2. The Stealer-Log Economy

Before getting into what Golliath collects, it's worth explaining what's actually being collected, because the volume and structure of everything downstream only makes sense in light of it.

An infostealer is consumer-grade malware: a small executable that, once it lands on a victim's machine (most often via cracked-software installers, fake browser updates, or malicious ad campaigns), reads whatever the browser and a handful of common applications have stored, credentials saved in the browser's password manager, session cookies, autofill form data, and a basic system fingerprint (hostname, IP, OS, installed hardware). It bundles all of that into an archive named after the victim session, then either uploads it to the operator's own panel or drops it straight into a Telegram channel.

The economics that make this worth automating at all are structural, not incidental. Stealers are usually rented rather than bought outright: the malware author maintains a builder and a back-end panel and sells access to affiliates on a subscription basis, the malware-as-a-service (MaaS) model that has dominated this space for years. The affiliate does the work of distribution; the operator collects a cut, either as a subscription fee or as a share of whatever the affiliate resells from the logs it generates.

What happens to a log pack after collection depends on where it sits in the resale chain. Some operators post logs publicly and for free, mainly to build a reputation and an audience for paid offerings later. Others sell access to a "cloud", a searchable, continuously updated database of stolen credentials, billed like a subscription rather than a one-time purchase. A separate tier sells static one-off packs aimed at buyers who want a specific batch rather than ongoing access. And underneath all of that sits a resale layer of aggregators: channels that run no stealer infrastructure of their own, but buy logs in bulk from multiple operators, strip the identifying metadata, rebrand the archive under a new name, and resell it as their own product. Section 13 covers how Golliath detects this rebranding.

None of this requires a single targeted victim to be valuable. The model depends on volume: a stealer doesn't care who it infects, and a marketplace doesn't care which operator's logs it's reselling, as long as flow keeps buyers coming back. That's the same property that makes the channels worth scraping at scale instead of monitoring by hand, the signal is in the aggregate, not in any single archive.

## 5. Scraping at Scale

### 5.1 The Session Pool

Telegram's MTProto API does not expose a simple REST endpoint; you authenticate as a user account (or bot) and maintain a session. The **session-manager** service (Python/Telethon) maintains a pool of authenticated sessions and exposes a small HTTP API that the Go services call.

Scraping a channel means calling `iter_messages` with a checkpoint cursor:

```python
async for message in client.iter_messages(
    entity,
    min_id=checkpoint_id,
    reverse=True,
    limit=3000,
):
    await publish_to_worker(message)
```

`reverse=True` walks forward from the checkpoint, so resuming a scrape after a restart does not re-process old messages. The checkpoint is stored in PostgreSQL and updated atomically after each batch commits to Kafka.

File downloads use the same session pool. Telethon streams file data in chunks; the session-manager relays those chunks to the **download-worker** over HTTP. I settled on 512 KB chunks after testing: larger chunks caused session timeouts on slow connections; smaller chunks introduced too much HTTP overhead per file.

### 5.2 Job Dispatch with SKIP LOCKED

The download-worker claims scrape jobs from PostgreSQL using a pattern that avoids distributed locks:

```sql
SELECT id, source_id, channel_identifier
FROM scrape_jobs
WHERE status = 'pending'
  AND scheduled_at <= NOW()
ORDER BY priority DESC, scheduled_at ASC
FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED
LIMIT 1;
```

`SKIP LOCKED` means multiple workers can race for jobs without blocking each other. Jobs that are locked by another worker are transparently skipped. This gives horizontal scalability for free: spin up more download-worker replicas and the queue drains faster with zero coordination overhead.

### 5.3 Kafka and File Priority Tiers

Once a message is fetched, the download-worker publishes it to the `messages.discovered` Kafka topic. Files attached to that message get routed to one of two priority topics:

- `files.priority`: text files (credential dumps, plain-text logs)
- `files.archives`: ZIP, RAR, 7z, TAR

The file-worker consumes these topics in priority order. Text files land in MinIO's `txt-dumps/` bucket for immediate parsing; archives go to `archives/`; executables and unknown formats are quarantined. SHA-256 is computed at download time and stored in PostgreSQL; duplicate files are detected before they are written, keeping the bucket clean.

### 5.4 Hunting Invite Links

Telegram invite links (`t.me/+HASH`) appear constantly in channel messages: operators cross-post content, advertise related services, and link to downstream distribution channels. The **message-indexer** extracts every invite link from indexed messages using a regex that covers all common patterns:

```go
var reInviteLink = regexp.MustCompile(
    `(?i)https?://(?:t\.me|telegram\.me|telegram\.dog)/(?:\+|joinchat/)([A-Za-z0-9_\-]{10,})`)
```

Matched hashes are stored in a PostgreSQL `invite_links` table with their source context. The frontend's `/hunt` view resolves these hashes via `CheckChatInviteRequest`, Telegram's RPC for probing private channel metadata without joining. This surfaces channel title, member count, description, and admin signatures from a hash alone.

The result is a ranked expansion surface: a small channel under monitoring often contains invite links to a much larger aggregator or supply channel that was never manually added to the source registry. One invite-link pivot in the right direction can add 10 new monitored sources.

## 6. Message Indexing and LLM-Assisted Channel Categorization

### 6.1 Real-Time Indexing

The **message-indexer** service (Go) consumes the `messages.discovered` Kafka topic and writes every message to OpenSearch. At 1,958.7 messages/second, new content from any monitored channel is searchable within seconds of being published.

After 2,208,360 indexed messages (2.2M+) across the monitored source set, the OpenSearch index becomes a live intelligence feed: analysts can query for a specific domain, an IP range, a malware family name, or a channel handle and get ranked results with full message context.

### 6.2 Sampling and LLM Pre-Labeling

Two separate sampling pipelines run against this corpus, and it's easy to conflate them since both start with "grab some content, send it to an LLM." They solve different problems.

**Channel categorization.** Manually reviewing hundreds of messages per source to decide its category does not scale. Golliath samples a random subset of *messages* from each unclassified source (typically 20-50) and passes them to **Gemini 2.5 Flash**, which returns a category guess for the source, logs, chatter, ULP, combos, marketplace, and so on, along with the signal it used to decide. A human analyst reviews the labels in Doccano, correcting errors; the labeled set trains a Gradient Boosting Classifier (85/15 train/test split, confidence threshold 0.70) that auto-categorizes future sources. This is the step that reduced manual categorization effort by roughly 80%; the residual ~20% (sources where classifier confidence falls below threshold or the content is ambiguous) still go to human review.

**Field extraction sampling.** Separately, Golliath samples raw `.txt` files from the same corpus, not Telegram messages, and asks Gemini to extract structured fields per line: URL, USERNAME, PASSWORD, DOMAIN, SUBDOMAIN. The prompt handles all four common credential delimiters, `email:password`, `url:user:pass`, `url|user|pass`, and variants where the password itself contains a colon (joined as trailing fields), and returns a strict JSON array with no markdown. Labels for the hardest 1,000 lines were fully reviewed by hand before being committed to the training set.

This sampling effort exists for a different reason than channel categorization. ULP (URL:login:password) combo lists are the messiest input format Golliath parses, and the four-format password cascade described later in Field Extractors can't anticipate every delimiter variant, encoding quirk, or field order a given combo-list seller uses. When the cascade silently misparses a line, a delimiter swallowed mid-password, a field shifted into the wrong column, the cascade itself has no way to know it got the columns wrong; it just produces a record that looks valid but isn't. The LLM-labeled sample is what catches that, because it labels the same lines independently and disagreements surface the misparse. The labeled dataset doubles as training data for the NLP models used elsewhere in extraction (the alias-mapping behind System Info normalization, free-text field recognition), and as a standing regression set: whenever a new ULP variant from a combo seller breaks the cascade, the next labeled batch usually contains examples of it, surfacing the gap before it silently degrades a whole source's data quality.

Once an identifier is confirmed correct, enrichment splits into two paths. The log parser itself enriches cheaply and in-line, with no network call: TLD validation, case normalization, the alias map. Anything that needs an external opinion, is this domain currently live, does this username show up elsewhere, gets queued to a third-party service instead. Both paths write back to the same identifier record, so a credential enriched in-process and one enriched by an external lookup end up in the same shape downstream.

## 7. The Archive Problem

Open any stealer-log archive and you will find a surprisingly consistent internal structure. The stealer client runs on the victim machine, collects credentials from browsers, writes everything to a local directory, and then compresses it before exfiltrating to the C2 server or dropping it directly to a Telegram channel.

Each stealer family has evolved its own layout convention. A Lumma archive looks like this:

```
LummaC2_2024_01_15_SESSION_12345/
├── domain_detect.txt          <- family fingerprint
├── Passwords/
│   ├── Chrome_Default.txt
│   └── Firefox.txt
├── Cookies/
│   ├── Chrome_Default.txt
│   └── network.txt
├── Autofill/
│   └── Chrome_Default.txt
└── System Info.txt
```

A WhiteSnake archive looks entirely different:

```
System_HOSTNAME_WIN10PRO/
├── Browsers/
│   ├── Passwords/
│   └── Cookies/
├── Cookies/                   <- root-level cookies too
└── System.txt
```

These are not cosmetic differences. A parser that assumes Lumma layout on a WhiteSnake archive will misroute the password files and produce nothing. Before I could write a single extractor, I needed a way to identify the family and select the right parsing logic.

## 8. The Parser Pipeline

The **parser_logs** subsystem implements a six-stage pipeline. Archives go in, structured JSON records come out. End-to-end, the pipeline sustains roughly 10.5 MB/s of archive throughput; the bottleneck is decompression and file-tree classification, not the per-role extractors downstream.

Archives don't only arrive via the Telegram scraper, either. Analysts regularly find credential dumps on forums, paste sites, or other sources outside the monitored channel set, and Golliath lets them drop those files straight into the same intake buckets for parsing. A manually submitted file is walked, classified, and routed through the exact same six stages as anything the scraper found; there is no separate code path for "things a human found" versus "things the scraper found."

### 8.1 Safety Before Parsing

The walker enforces hard limits before extracting a single byte of content. This matters because attackers have started distributing archive bombs (intentionally malformed archives that expand to hundreds of gigabytes) and zip-slip payloads that try to write outside the extraction directory.

```python
ARCHIVE_BOMB_RATIO = 20        # compressed:uncompressed > 20x -> abort
ARCHIVE_BOMB_MIN_SIZE = 1_048_576  # only apply ratio check above 1 MB
MAX_ENTRY_COUNT = 100_000
MAX_NESTED_DEPTH = 3
```

Zip-slip protection resolves all paths relative to the extraction root and rejects any entry whose resolved path escapes it:

```python
def _safe_extract_path(archive_root: Path, entry_name: str) -> Path:
    target = (archive_root / entry_name).resolve()
    if not str(target).startswith(str(archive_root.resolve())):
        raise ZipSlipError(f"Attempted path traversal: {entry_name}")
    return target
```

These are not theoretical risks. Zip-slip payloads appear in the wild corpus.

### 8.2 Layout Classification and Grammar Selection

After safe extraction, the **layout classifier** surveys the directory tree without reading file contents. It looks for family-specific structural signatures:

- `domain_detect.txt` or `domaindetect.txt` at root: **Lumma** signal
- `Network/` containing `UserName_*` subdirectories: **RisePro** signal
- `System_HOSTNAME/` directories at root: **WhiteSnake** signal
- `log.txt` at root with a `Passwords/` sibling: **RedLine** signal

These signals are passed to the **GrammarRouter**, which calls `matches()` on every registered grammar and picks the highest scorer above 0.60:

```python
class GrammarRouter:
    def __init__(self, grammars: list[FamilyGrammar]) -> None:
        self._grammars = sorted(grammars, key=lambda g: g.priority, reverse=True)

    def route(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> FamilyGrammar:
        scores = [(g, g.matches(layout)) for g in self._grammars]
        best_grammar, best_score = max(scores, key=lambda x: x[1])
        if best_score >= CONFIDENCE_THRESHOLD:
            return best_grammar
        return self._generic  # unconditional fallback
```

<GolliathGrammarRouter />

The threshold of 0.60 was chosen empirically: below it, the grammar was wrong more often than the generic fallback would be. Tie-breaking favors more-specific grammars; `GenericGrammar` always returns 0.0, so it never wins a competition.

## 9. Family Grammars

Every stealer family gets its own `FamilyGrammar` subclass. The abstract base defines a simple contract:

<CollapsibleCode title="FamilyGrammar ABC" language="python">
```python
from abc import ABC, abstractmethod
from dataclasses import dataclass
from pathlib import Path

@dataclass
class ArchiveLayout:
    root: Path
    entries: list[str]
    file_tree: dict[str, list[Path]]

class FamilyGrammar(ABC):
    priority: int = 0  # higher = tried first in sort order

    @abstractmethod
    def matches(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> float:
        """Return confidence 0.0-1.0. Above 0.60 wins routing."""
        ...

    @abstractmethod
    def extract(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> SessionRecord:
        """Parse the layout and return a structured session record."""
        ...
```
</CollapsibleCode>

### 9.1 LummaGrammar

Lumma is the dominant stealer-as-a-service family in the current corpus. Its archives are identified by `domain_detect.txt`, a list of domains that the stealer was configured to target, one per line.

<CollapsibleCode title="LummaGrammar.matches()" language="python">
```python
class LummaGrammar(FamilyGrammar):
    priority = 80

    def matches(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> float:
        has_domain_detect = any(
            e.lower() in ("domain_detect.txt", "domaindetect.txt")
            for e in layout.entries
        )
        if not has_domain_detect:
            return 0.05
        has_passwords = "Passwords" in layout.file_tree
        has_cookies = "Cookies" in layout.file_tree
        score = 0.70
        if has_passwords:
            score += 0.07
        if has_cookies:
            score += 0.05
        return min(score, 1.0)
```
</CollapsibleCode>

The `domain_detect.txt` file is also intelligence in itself: it reveals which domains the affiliate configured the stealer to prioritize, often including specific banking, crypto exchange, and corporate SSO domains.

### 9.2 RiseProGrammar

RisePro organizes each victim session into a named subdirectory under `Network/`. The directory name encodes a username hash: `Network/UserName_a3f9b2/`. This layout is distinctive enough that the classifier scores it at ~0.88.

<CollapsibleCode title="RiseProGrammar.matches()" language="python">
```python
class RiseProGrammar(FamilyGrammar):
    priority = 88

    _USERNAME_PATTERN = re.compile(r"^UserName_[A-Za-z0-9]+$")

    def matches(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> float:
        network_children = layout.file_tree.get("Network", [])
        username_dirs = [
            p for p in network_children
            if p.is_dir() and self._USERNAME_PATTERN.match(p.name)
        ]
        if not username_dirs:
            return 0.04
        ratio = len(username_dirs) / max(len(network_children), 1)
        return 0.75 + (0.13 * ratio)
```
</CollapsibleCode>

### 9.3 WhiteSnakeGrammar

WhiteSnake is the highest-confidence detection in the registry. Its signature stacks three independent signals rather than relying on a single file:

<CollapsibleCode title="WhiteSnakeGrammar.matches()" language="python">
```python
class WhiteSnakeGrammar(FamilyGrammar):
    priority = 92

    def matches(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> float:
        has_system_dir = any(e.startswith("System_") for e in layout.entries)
        if not has_system_dir:
            return 0.03
        has_root_cookies = "Cookies" in layout.file_tree
        score = 0.85
        if has_root_cookies:
            score += 0.10
        return min(score, 0.95)
```
</CollapsibleCode>

`System_HOSTNAME/` directories (one per victim session in aggregator repacks) are the strong signal here; a root-level `Cookies/` directory co-occurring with them is what pushes confidence the rest of the way to 0.95.

### 9.4 RedLineGrammar

RedLine sessions are identified by a `log.txt` metadata header at the session root sitting next to a `Passwords/` directory of browser-named text files.

<CollapsibleCode title="RedLineGrammar.matches()" language="python">
```python
class RedLineGrammar(FamilyGrammar):
    priority = 75

    def matches(self, layout: ArchiveLayout) -> float:
        has_log_txt = "log.txt" in (e.lower() for e in layout.entries)
        has_passwords_dir = "Passwords" in layout.file_tree
        if not (has_log_txt and has_passwords_dir):
            return 0.04
        score = 0.70
        if "Autofill" in layout.file_tree or "CreditCards" in layout.file_tree:
            score += 0.08
        return min(score, 0.78)
```
</CollapsibleCode>

RedLine has been through more public version changes than any other family in the corpus. Somewhere around v22, the password format switched from key:value blocks to pipe-separated lines, which is exactly why the password cascade in Field Extractors tries pipe-separated before falling back to the older colon-separated format: the grammar has to account for a family that doesn't agree with its own past versions.

### 9.5 GenericGrammar (The Fallback)

When no family grammar scores above 0.60, `GenericGrammar` takes over. It runs all four password format parsers in cascade and tries the best-effort cookie parser. Coverage is lower than a matched grammar, but it is still far better than discarding the archive entirely.

Across one benchmark run against a real-world corpus, the router correctly identified the family for 85% of archives. The remaining 15% fell through to `GenericGrammar`, which still extracted credentials and cookies from most of them, just with lower field completeness than a matched grammar would have produced. In practice, that 15% is dominated by aggregator repacks that strip or rename family-specific files before redistribution (more on that in Section 13), not by genuinely novel stealer formats, which is why a high fallback rate alone is never enough on its own to say a new family has shown up in the corpus. Section 11 covers how Golliath actually tells the difference.

### 9.6 Comparing the Five Grammars

| Grammar | Priority | Key Signal | Max Confidence | Formats Handled |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| WhiteSnakeGrammar | 92 | `System_HOSTNAME/` + root `Cookies/` | 0.95 | colon-separated, cookies, autofill, credit cards |
| RiseProGrammar | 88 | `Network/UserName_*` dirs | 0.88 | TSV, cookies, system info |
| LummaGrammar | 80 | `domain_detect.txt` | 0.82 | key:value, cookies, autofill, system info |
| RedLineGrammar | 75 | `log.txt` + `Passwords/` | 0.78 | pipe-separated, cookies, autofill, credit cards |
| GenericGrammar | 0 (fallback) | none, unconditional 0.0 | n/a | all four password formats, best-effort cookies |

New grammars get added the same way these five were: someone notices a recurring layout that `GenericGrammar` is handling poorly, writes a `matches()` / `extract()` pair, and registers it with a priority. The harder problem is noticing the recurring layout in the first place when you're looking at a few hundred fallback archives a day. That's what the clustering pipeline in Section 11 is for.

## 10. Field Extractors

Once the grammar routes the archive, per-role extractors run against the classified file list. The most complex is the **password extractor**, which handles four distinct formats across stealer families.

### 10.1 Password Format Cascade

Passwords are parsed in priority order, stopping at the first format that yields results:

**1. Key:value blocks** (Lumma, some RedLine variants):
```
URL: https://example.com/login
UserName: alice@corp.com
Password: hunter2

URL: https://bank.example/auth
...
```

**2. TSV format** (RisePro):
```
https://mail.google.com	alice@gmail.com	••••••••
https://github.com	alice	gh_token_abc123
```

**3. Pipe-separated** (RedLine v22+):
```
https://twitter.com/|alice|P@ssw0rd!
```

**4. Colon-separated** (WhiteSnake, generic):
```
https://corp.okta.com:alice@corp.com:S3cur3P@ss
```

Each format parser is implemented as a standalone function that returns `list[PasswordRecord] | None`. The cascade tries them in order and short-circuits on the first non-empty result. If all four fail, the file is logged as unparseable and added to the backlog, Section 12 covers what happens to that backlog. Across one benchmark run against a real-world corpus, this cascade resolved 314,717 individual credential records.

### 10.2 Cookie Parsing

Cookies follow the Netscape 7-column TSV format that browsers export. The parser enforces strict column counts; the most common corruption in wild archives is lines with missing tab separators, which a lenient parser would silently misparse into wrong fields.

```python
NETSCAPE_COLUMNS = 7

def parse_netscape_cookies(text: str) -> list[CookieRecord]:
    records = []
    for line in text.splitlines():
        if line.startswith("#") or not line.strip():
            continue
        cols = line.split("\t")
        if len(cols) != NETSCAPE_COLUMNS:
            continue  # log and skip, do not attempt partial parsing
        domain, include_subdomains, path, secure, expiry, name, value = cols
        records.append(CookieRecord(
            domain=domain.lstrip("."),
            path=path,
            name=name,
            value=value,
            secure=secure.upper() == "TRUE",
            http_only=False,
            expires=int(expiry) if expiry.isdigit() else None,
        ))
    return records
```

The 14.9 million cookies in the benchmark corpus made this the highest-volume extractor by a wide margin. Session cookies are the primary target: a valid session cookie bypasses MFA entirely.

### 10.3 System Info and the Alias Map

Every stealer writes a system fingerprint file, but the field names are not standardized. Lumma calls it `System Info.txt` with `IP: 1.2.3.4` lines; WhiteSnake uses `System.txt` with `Ip =` lines; RedLine uses `log.txt` with `IP Address:` lines.

The system info extractor maintains an alias map:

```python
FIELD_ALIASES: dict[str, list[str]] = {
    "hostname": ["computer name", "hostname", "pc name", "computername"],
    "ip": ["ip", "ip address", "external ip", "wan ip"],
    "os": ["os", "operating system", "windows version"],
    "hardware": ["hardware", "cpu", "gpu", "ram", "processor"],
    "country": ["country", "location", "geo"],
    "hwid": ["hwid", "machine id", "hardware id", "uuid"],
}
```

Normalization means downstream queries (`WHERE system_info.country = 'US'`) work regardless of which stealer family produced the record.

### 10.4 Autofill and Payment Card Records

Autofill and payment-card extraction are simpler than passwords or cookies because browsers export both as flat, already-structured data rather than free text. Autofill records are name/value pairs straight from the browser's form-fill store (`email -> alice@corp.com`, `shipping_zip -> 10001`); the extractor does little beyond grouping them per session. Payment-card records are matched with a regex for a 13-19 digit PAN alongside an adjacent expiry and CVV field; structural position, which lines sit near which other lines, does most of the disambiguation work, since a bare digit-count regex alone would catch unrelated numeric strings.

In the same benchmark run referenced above, this surfaced 28,409 autofill records and 1,204 payment-card records.

## 11. Discovering New Stealer Families via Clustering

The 15% of archives that fall through to `GenericGrammar` aren't just overflow to tolerate. They're also where the next family lives before anyone has named it. Underground stealer development moves fast: builders fork, forks get rebranded, rebrands get sold under a new name on a different channel. By the time a "new" stealer gets a name in public threat reporting, an unnamed variant may already have been circulating in the corpus for weeks, quietly falling through to the generic parser the entire time.

The approach is to stop treating every fallback archive as an independent failure and instead look at them as a population. Each fallback archive gets embedded, not at the credential level, but at the structural level: directory depth, file-name n-grams, the order in which roles appear, the presence or absence of specific markers (a `domain_detect.txt`-shaped file under a different name, say). Those structural feature vectors get clustered with HDBSCAN, the same density-based clustering Golliath already relies on elsewhere in the pipeline, on multilingual embeddings so that a Russian-language variant and an English-language variant of the same underlying builder still land in the same cluster.

A cluster that's large enough and tight enough, dozens of archives sharing an unusual but consistent layout, is worth a human look. Two outcomes follow from that look. Either it's a genuine new stealer family or a rebrand significant enough to warrant its own `FamilyGrammar`, in which case the fix is writing a new `matches()` / `extract()` pair and registering it the way the five in Section 9 were. Or it's an existing family whose builder changed, a file got renamed, a folder got moved, in which case the fix is usually a one-line addition to an existing grammar's signal set rather than a whole new grammar.

Either way, clustering turns "format we can't parse" into "format we haven't named yet," which is a far more actionable backlog than a pile of generic-fallback failures, and it's the closest thing Golliath has to spotting a builder fork before a security vendor publishes a name for it.

## 12. AI-Assisted Triage for the Dead Letter Queue

Every extractor cascade in Section 10 ends the same way: if nothing matches, the file is logged as unparseable and written to a backlog. I call it a DLQ, borrowing the Kafka term, even though the actual implementation is a PostgreSQL table rather than a Kafka topic, the failure happens deep inside batch processing, not at message-consumption time.

Historically that backlog just grew. Someone would periodically skim a sample, decide whether anything in it mattered, and most of it aged out unread. The fix wasn't a smarter regex, it was applying the same Gemini-based pre-labeling from Section 6.2 to the DLQ itself:

1. Batch DLQ entries by archive rather than by file, so the model sees an unparsed file in the context of its siblings: the system info file that succeeded, the layout that almost matched a grammar.
2. Ask the model a narrower question than full extraction: is this a credential format with no parser yet, a known format that's corrupted, or junk (channel promotion, filler text, non-stealer content)?
3. Route the answer. "No parser yet" items feed into the clustering pipeline in Section 11. "Corrupted" items get tagged with a specific failure signature (truncated, wrong encoding, missing delimiter) so a human can decide whether it's worth a defensive fix. "Junk" gets discarded with the classification reason recorded, closing the loop without a human ever opening the file.

This doesn't replace the cascade parsers, it triages what's left after they fail, at a volume no analyst could review line by line, and it turns the DLQ from a write-only graveyard into the intake queue for both grammar coverage gaps and new-family discovery.

## 13. The Aggregator Problem

A significant fraction of Telegram stealer-log channels are not operators; they are **aggregators**. They buy log packs from multiple stealer networks, strip identifying metadata, rebrand the archives, and resell them to credential stuffers.

This matters for two reasons. First, the rebranding often removes or renames the family-specific files (like `domain_detect.txt`), pushing more archives into the generic fallback. Second, aggregators sometimes use the password field itself as advertising space:

```
URL: https://mail.google.com
UserName: alice@gmail.com
Password: JOIN @BESTLOGS FOR MORE  <- channel promotion, not a credential
```

The extractor detects and discards these entries using a heuristic: if the password field matches a `@` handle, a `t.me/` URL, or common promotional phrases, the record is flagged and excluded from the credential index.

Known aggregator brands encountered in the corpus: **Exodus**, **AzoriX**, **MOAB Stealer**, **PureLog**, **Shadow Logs**, **UnknownStealer**, **Meduza**, **StealC** repacks, and several unnamed ones identified only by watermark patterns.

The watermark removal is a separate post's worth of content. In short: most watermarks are added as directory name prefixes (`[EXODUS] SESSION_123/`) or injected into `System Info.txt` headers. Both are stripped during layout classification before the grammar sees them.

## 14. Intelligence at the Top

With parsed data in OpenSearch and the relationship graph in Neo4j, meaningful intelligence queries become fast.

### 14.1 Domain Exposure Reports

The most common operational query: "Is domain X in the credential set?" Two-pass OpenSearch aggregation:

1. First pass: count sessions containing credentials for the target domain
2. Second pass: enumerate unique username/password pairs, deduplicated by credential hash

Results feed directly into the `/hunt` API endpoint and surface in the frontend's domain search.

### 14.2 Channel Topology via Neo4j

The graph model captures how threat actor infrastructure connects:

```cypher
MATCH (a:Source)-[:FORWARDED_FROM_SOURCE]->(b:Source)
WHERE b.identifier = '@target_channel'
RETURN a.title, a.member_count, a.created_at
ORDER BY a.member_count DESC
```

This surfaces every channel that has forwarded content from a target channel, revealing distribution networks and shared admin infrastructure that is invisible from message-level analysis.

### 14.3 Actor Pivoting

```cypher
MATCH (u:TelegramUser)-[:ACTIVE_IN]->(s:Source)
WHERE s.identifier IN ['@chan_a', '@chan_b']
WITH u, collect(s.identifier) AS channels
WHERE size(channels) > 1
RETURN u.username, channels
```

A user active in multiple monitored channels is a pivot point, potentially an operator, reseller, or admin cross-posting content.

### 14.4 Custom Pipelines and Triggers

Both queries above are things an analyst runs by hand when they think to ask. Golliath also lets you describe standing pipelines that run automatically: a `trigger -> condition -> action` rule evaluated against the live event stream instead of a one-off query. The simplest form is a message trigger: when a new message lands on channel X (or matches a pattern), run action Y, anything from a webhook notification to kicking off a scrape of a linked channel to writing a new edge into the graph.

The most useful version of this builds directly on actor pivoting. Rather than running the Cypher query in 14.3 manually after the fact, you describe a standing pipeline: whenever a `TelegramUser` is seen posting in a newly monitored channel, check whether that username has prior activity tied to the same identity on any other monitored source, including non-Telegram forums where the username has been seen, and emit a pivot event if it does. Usernames get reused across both Telegram channels and the dark-web forums those channels advertise, a vendor's forum handle frequently matches their Telegram handle exactly, since reputation is the product being sold, so this turns a one-off "who else has this user been active with" query into something that fires automatically the moment a new account surfaces anywhere in the monitored set.

Pipelines are defined declaratively, trigger plus condition plus action, rather than as bespoke code per use case, so adding a new one is a configuration change, not a deploy.

## 15. TLD Analytics and Geographic Density

After roughly a month of continuous parsing, deduplicating repeated archive releases across that window brings the corpus to **315M+ unique credentials** across 60 TLDs, revealing a clear geographic picture of who is being targeted. The `.com` namespace dominates at 148M credentials, unsurprising given that most global services use `.com` domains. Among country-code TLDs, Brazil leads by a wide margin (7.1M), followed by India (4.8M) and Indonesia (3.6M).

The per-capita view is more revealing. When normalized against population, smaller countries can surface as disproportionately targeted. Countries in Latin America (Peru, Chile) and Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Indonesia) rank higher per-capita than their absolute numbers suggest, consistent with the known geographic distribution of stealer-malware campaigns that favor regions with high smartphone penetration but lower security awareness.

## What's Next

The parser backlog has a few open items: **email extraction** (deferred because regexes produce too many false positives without a verification step), **browser history parsing** (the files exist in most archives but are not yet structured), and **MLflow integration** for tracking grammar performance across corpus versions.

The DLQ triage in Section 12 currently stops at classification. The next step is closing the loop further: having the same model draft a first-pass `matches()` / `extract()` stub from a discovered cluster (Section 11), for a human to review and register rather than write from scratch. That turns "we found a new family" into "here's a grammar for it" in one motion instead of two.

On the collection side, I want to add **reaction-weighted scrape priority**: channels where file posts get heavy reactions (indicating active buyers) should be scraped more aggressively than quiet channels. The custom-pipeline system in Section 14.4 is also still message-trigger only; extending it to file-level triggers (when an archive matching a known cluster fingerprint lands, auto-tag and notify) is the natural next step now that the clustering pipeline exists to produce those fingerprints.

The frontend's `/explorer` view is partially built; it can display sessions and credentials but does not yet surface the new-family cluster view or the actor-pivot graph inline. That is the next frontend sprint.
