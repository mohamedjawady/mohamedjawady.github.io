> **TIP Focus:** A threat intelligence platform is infrastructure, not intelligence. This post is about the infrastructure: the data model that turns a pile of indicators into something an analyst can correlate, trust, and share.

**TL;DR:** A <Term concept="tip">threat intelligence platform</Term> is not a blocklist and it does not produce intelligence for you. Its job is to store the analysis behind each indicator, correlate it automatically against everything else you and your sharing partners have entered, and let other tools query it over an API. MISP does this with a small, learnable data model: <Term concept="misp-event">events</Term> hold <Term concept="misp-attribute">attributes</Term>, attributes carry a category and a type, <Term concept="sighting">sightings</Term> turn a static indicator into something with a live reputation, and classification happens in three layers of increasing structure (tags, taxonomies, and <Term concept="misp-galaxy">galaxies</Term>) that most new users flatten into one thing and shouldn't. This post walks the model end to end and builds one event by hand.

## Introduction

Every SOC eventually accumulates a spreadsheet, or a `blocklist.txt`, or a saved search: some ad hoc place where "IPs and domains we know are bad" get written down. It works for a while. Then someone asks whether IP `1.2.3.4` from six weeks ago is the same infrastructure as the one in this morning's alert, and the honest answer is nobody remembers, and nothing in the spreadsheet would tell them even if they did. That gap, between *having* indicators and being able to *use* them, is exactly what a threat intelligence platform exists to close.

This blog has already covered the intelligence side of that pipeline: what CTI actually is, how to threat model who's worth watching, and why raw indicators make a weak handoff to detection engineering. This post is about the piece underneath all three: the system of record that stores the analysis those posts assume already happened, and makes it queryable instead of tribal knowledge. MISP, the Malware Information Sharing Platform, is the reference implementation, free, open source, and the closest thing the field has to a de facto standard. Understanding its data model is understanding what any TIP has to get right.

## What a TIP Actually Has to Do

Strip away the product marketing and a TIP has four jobs, and most of the disappointment people report with early-generation tools traces back to one of these being weak.

First, it has to store more than a list. "IP `1.2.3.4` is bad" is nearly useless on its own. "IP `1.2.3.4` resolved to `evilsite.com` and served an exploit kit on 2018-02-19, observed during triage of case #4471" is something an analyst can act on and reason about six months later. The context (when, how, in what campaign, with what confidence) is the actual product; the raw value is just the key you look it up by.

Second, it has to correlate automatically. If two unrelated-looking phishing emails share a file hash, a human should not have to notice that by memory. The platform should surface the link the moment the second one is entered, because that link is often the entire difference between "two isolated incidents" and "one campaign."

Third, it has to be queryable by machines, not just people. If your <Term concept="siem">SIEM</Term>, your case management system, and your automation platform can't ask the TIP "have you seen this before" over an API in milliseconds, the TIP becomes one more browser tab analysts have to remember to check, and in practice they won't, every time.

Fourth, for many teams, it has to support sharing indicators and context with other organizations, because a threat that hit your peer last week is a threat you have a head start on if their platform can talk to yours.

None of these four are things a spreadsheet, a wiki page, or a plain blocklist can do well, which is the actual argument for a dedicated platform.

## The MISP Data Model

MISP organizes everything around one core idea: the <Term concept="misp-event">event</Term>. Every report you read, every sample you analyze, every incident you want to track becomes one event: a container with a unique ID that holds a group of typed pieces of data underneath it.

Those pieces of data are <Term concept="misp-attribute">attributes</Term>. An attribute is the actual indicator or artifact: a hash, a domain, a URL, an IP, or even a block of explanatory text or an attached file. Every attribute carries two labels. The **category** describes how it was used in the intrusion (payload delivery, artifacts dropped, payload installation, external analysis, network activity) and the **type** describes what kind of data it literally is: `md5`, `sha256`, `domain`, `url`, `email-subject`, dozens more. The pairing matters: an `md5` under "payload delivery" and an `md5` under "payload installation" are the same data type playing two different roles in the story of the attack, and keeping that distinction is what lets you later ask "which stage of delivery are we weakest at detecting" instead of just "which hashes have we seen."

Attributes that are identical across different events get flagged for correlation automatically. This is the mechanism, not a feature bolted on top. Enter the same hash into two events created weeks apart by two different analysts, and MISP surfaces the link without either of them having to remember or search for it.

## Sightings: Reputation That Updates Instead of a List That Rots

A blocklist is frozen the moment it's written. An entry either sits there forever looking equally authoritative, or someone remembers to prune it, which in practice nobody does reliably. MISP's answer is <Term concept="sighting">sightings</Term>: a lightweight mechanism where any analyst, anywhere in the sharing community, can record "I saw this attribute, and it was a true positive" or "I saw this attribute, and it was noise."

This sounds small, but it changes what an indicator *is*. A hash entered a year ago with zero sightings since is a different object than one sighted fourteen times last quarter, even though both display the same value. The first is stale and worth a second look before you trust it; the second has been battle-tested by real triage across possibly multiple organizations. Sightings are what let a TIP represent "confidence earned through use" instead of "confidence asserted once at ingest and never revisited," and they are the mechanism that should feed the vetting step in an indicator's <Term concept="indicator-lifecycle">lifecycle</Term>, which is the piece a lot of teams skip under volume pressure. An indicator with strong negative sighting history is a candidate for retirement whether or not anyone remembers to look.

## Three Layers of Classification, Not One

New MISP users consistently flatten tags, taxonomies, and galaxies into "the labeling thing," and then wonder why the galaxy view behaves differently from the tag filter. They are three distinct tools stacked in order of structure, and picking the right one for a given classification job is worth getting right.

**Tags** are arbitrary strings attached at the event level. Nothing stops you from typing anything: `follow-up-needed`, `q3-campaign`, whatever your team finds useful. They carry zero built-in meaning; they're a label, full stop.

**Taxonomies** are pre-made vocabularies of tags, shipped or shared as a set, so `kill-chain:delivery` and `kill-chain:installation` mean the same thing to every analyst who uses them instead of six slightly different spellings of the same idea. A taxonomy constrains tags into a shared language without adding any data beyond the string itself.

**Galaxies** are the layer with actual substance. A galaxy is a family of *clusters*, say a "threat actor" galaxy containing one cluster per known group, and each cluster can hold its own notes, synonyms, and attributes, independent of any single event. This is the structural difference that matters: tag an event `apt28` and you've labeled that one event. Attach the Sandworm galaxy cluster and you've linked the event into a standing, cross-event body of knowledge about that actor: their known synonyms (Sandworm has been called TeleBots and VoodooBear by different vendors), their associated tooling, and every other event anyone has ever clustered against them. Galaxies are how MISP tracks actors, malware families, and TTPs as durable entities that accumulate evidence over time, rather than as a string that happens to be spelled the same way twice.

| Layer | What it is | What it's for |
|---|---|---|
| Tag | An arbitrary string on an event | Ad hoc, team-specific labeling |
| Taxonomy | A shared, pre-defined vocabulary of tags | Consistent terminology across analysts and orgs |
| Galaxy / cluster | A named entity with its own notes and attributes | Tracking actors, malware, and TTPs across events over time |

## Sharing: Why the Platform Gets Better With Neighbors

MISP was built for inter-organizational sharing from the ground up, not retrofitted for it later, and that shows in the design. Each organization runs its own MISP instance and decides, per event, whether and how far that event should propagate: to a specific trusted partner, to a sharing community, or nowhere beyond the local instance. Linked instances then synchronize automatically, so an event published by one organization can appear, with its attributes and classification intact, on every instance downstream of it within minutes.

The classification that travels with a shared event matters as much as the indicators themselves. <Term concept="tlp">TLP</Term> tags (RED, AMBER, GREEN, WHITE/CLEAR) are usually applied as a taxonomy, and they tell every recipient how far they're permitted to redistribute what they just received. This is what makes sharing safe rather than reckless: the restriction travels with the data instead of depending on every recipient remembering a verbal agreement from six months ago.

The case for doing this at all is simple math. Every organization sees a slice of the threat landscape. A community of organizations sharing sighted, contextualized events sees a much larger slice than any one of them could collect alone, and the correlation engine described above works exactly as well on an externally-sourced event as on one you entered yourself, which is the whole point.

## A Word on Hygiene: Defanging

One small operational detail worth naming because it causes real incidents when skipped: many tools, chat clients, and note-taking apps auto-linkify anything that looks like a URL or IP address the moment it's typed or pasted. Paste a live malicious link into a ticket, a Slack message, or your own analysis notes, and someone, maybe you six months from now skimming old notes, can click it by accident.

<Term concept="defanging">Defanging</Term> avoids this by breaking the pattern recognition: `evilsite[.]com` instead of `evilsite.com`, `hxxp://` instead of `http://`. It's a small habit, but it's the kind of thing that should be a reflex any time an indicator gets typed somewhere outside the TIP itself, where structured fields make the auto-linkification risk moot.

In practice two paths run constantly. The automated path is highest-volume: a SIEM or SOAR platform queries the TIP's API on every alert to check whether an involved IP, domain, or hash is already known, and pushes new observables back in as it learns them. TheHive's integration with MISP is a clean example: when an analyst adds an observable to a case, it can create or update a MISP event automatically, so the TIP fills up as a byproduct of normal casework instead of requiring a second, separate data-entry step. The manual path is lower-volume but higher-value: an analyst reading a vendor report or finishing a deep investigation creates or enriches an event by hand, adding the context that no automated feed could infer: why this matters, what campaign it belongs to, what actor it clusters with.

## Building One Event, Start to Finish

Walk through the mechanics with a concrete example: turning a public write-up about a ransomware outbreak into a usable MISP event.

1. **Create the event.** Set the event date to when the activity actually occurred, not today; this matters for later timeline correlation. Title it descriptively; using the source report's own title is a common and sane convention.
2. **Tag for sharing sensitivity first.** Before adding a single indicator, apply the TLP tag. If the source is a public blog post, that's `tlp:white`: open information, safe to redistribute freely.
3. **Bulk-import attributes with free text.** Rather than hand-entering each hash and URL into its own field, paste the block of indicators from the report into MISP's free-text import tool. It auto-detects type per line (`sha256`, `url`, `domain`), faster and with fewer transcription errors than typing them one at a time.
4. **Correct categories.** Auto-detection gets the type right reliably; it can't know the *role* each indicator played. A hash needs to be marked as, say, "payload installation" rather than "external analysis," and that correction is where an analyst's judgment actually enters the record.
5. **Publish, and watch correlation happen automatically.** The moment the event is published, if any of its attributes match attributes already present, including ones pulled in automatically from a subscribed external feed like CIRCL's OSINT feed, MISP surfaces the correlation without being asked. A hash entered by hand can suddenly link your local event to a dozen externally-sourced events for the same campaign, each with its own additional context to pivot into.

That correlation step is the payoff for everything above it. A spreadsheet with the same three hashes in it would never have told you they connect to twelve other events from three other organizations. This is the entire argument for a platform over a list, made concrete.

## What a TIP Doesn't Do

Worth stating plainly, because it's the most common expectation mismatch when a team stands up MISP for the first time: **a TIP does not produce intelligence.** It stores and operationalizes intelligence that a human already produced through analysis. Feeding a TIP a firehose of unvetted external indicator feeds and calling that a threat intelligence program confuses inventory with analysis. It's the same failure mode covered in this blog's post on the gap between indicators and behavioral analytics, just one layer earlier in the pipeline. A MISP instance full of unsighted, unclassified, never-pivoted-on events is not meaningfully different from the spreadsheet it replaced; it's just a more expensive spreadsheet with a nicer search bar. The platform earns its keep only when the data going in already carries the analysis, and when sightings, correlation, and galaxies get used to keep that data alive instead of static.

## Conclusion

A threat intelligence platform's value is entirely in the model underneath it: whether it can hold context instead of just values, correlate automatically instead of relying on memory, expose that data to other tools over an API, and optionally extend all of it to trusted partners without leaking more than intended. MISP's event/attribute/tag/taxonomy/galaxy/sighting stack is a well-worn answer to all four requirements, and understanding it is what turns "we have a MISP instance" into "we have a threat intelligence platform that's actually being used as one."

For the analysis discipline that should produce what goes into these events, see this blog's post on analytic judgment. For what should happen after an indicator leaves the TIP and heads toward detection, see the post on indicators versus behavioral analytics: the TIP is the memory, and the transposition into durable detections is the next and harder step.
