> **Threat Modeling Focus:** There is threat intelligence available on nearly every threat that exists. Reading all of it is impossible and pointless. A threat model is the discipline that tells you which threats are plausibly yours, so you can spend finite attention on those and consciously ignore the rest.

**TL;DR:** A CTI <Term concept="threat-model">threat model</Term> answers "what should we actually worry about" by starting from your <Term concept="crown-jewels">crown jewels</Term>, not from a list of trending actors. You inventory what you have that is worth taking, map each asset to the adversary who would want it and why, and then pivot both ways until the model is deep enough to drive a decision. The payoff is the right to ignore things: most threats are universal noise handled by hygiene, and only a handful are targeted at you specifically. Threat modeling separates the two.

## Introduction

Ask a security leader what threats they worry about and you often get a list assembled from whatever crossed their feed this quarter: the ransomware crew in the headlines, the nation-state group a vendor just profiled, the vulnerability everyone is tweeting about. Some of it is relevant. Most of it is not, and there is no stated method for telling which is which.

That is the problem a threat model solves. There is credible reporting on essentially every threat in existence, and ingesting all of it is not diligence, it is drowning. A threat model is the filter: a documented, prioritized picture of which threats plausibly apply to your organization, built deliberately enough that you can defend the important ones and consciously set the rest aside. Sun Tzu's line gets quoted to death here, know the enemy and know yourself, but the ordering is the real lesson. You cannot know which enemy matters until you know what you have that is worth taking. This post builds a CTI threat model the way it should be built, from the inside out, using target-centric analysis and a single worked example.

## Universal Threats Versus Your Threats

Threats fall into two buckets, and conflating them is the most common failure in the whole discipline.

Universal threats hit anyone connected to the internet: commodity malware, indiscriminate phishing, mass vulnerability scanning, opportunistic credential stuffing. They are not about you. They are weather, and you dress for them with baseline hygiene: patching, MFA, email filtering, backups. Targeted threats are different. Someone selected you, because of what you make, what you hold, who your customers are, or what you represent. These are the threats a threat model exists to surface, because the response to them is specific rather than generic.

| | Universal threats | Targeted threats |
|---|---|---|
| Selection | You are on the internet | You were chosen, for a reason |
| Examples | Commodity malware, mass scanning, spray phishing | Tailored espionage, sector-specific extortion, a motivated insider |
| Right response | Baseline hygiene, handled generically | Specific intelligence, detection, and controls |
| Threat model's job | Confirm hygiene covers it, then move on | Identify, prioritize, and watch deliberately |

The trap is treating everything as targeted. A team that reads every nation-state report as though it describes their own adversary burns out chasing intelligence that was never about them, while the two or three actors who genuinely care about their crown jewels get the same diluted attention as the hundred who do not. The model's first job is to draw this line honestly.

## Threat Modeling Is Applied Systems Analysis

Before naming assets or adversaries, it helps to know what kind of activity this is. A <Term concept="systems-analysis">systems analysis</Term> is the deliberate construction of the mental models you will use to reason about something. A system, in the classic definition, is an orderly grouping of interdependent components working toward a goal, and analyzing one means understanding its parts well enough to reason about how a change in one ripples through the others.

Harold Leavitt's well-known model of an organization captures this with four interacting components, structure, tasks, people, and technology, sitting inside a field of forces that push them to change: internal forces like a reorganization, external ones like a new regulation, a technology shift, or social pressure. A full systems analysis of your organization across all four components is valuable and expensive. A threat model is the threat-focused slice of that analysis: you keep the parts of the system picture that matter for how an adversary would target you and set the rest aside.

One clarification prevents confusion with a different discipline that shares the name. Application security threat modeling, the STRIDE lineage, models a system's own weaknesses to find where it could be attacked. CTI threat modeling models the adversaries who would do the attacking and why. Both are useful and they meet in the middle, but this post is about the second.

## Start From the Crown Jewels, Not the Adversary

Here is the counterintuitive move at the center of the whole method: you are the first target in your own threat model. Before you name a single actor, you inventory what you have that someone would want. The instinct is to start with the scary actors and work toward your defenses, but that gets the causality backwards. Adversaries are not interested in you in the abstract. They are interested in something you have, so the something comes first.

Inventory your crown jewels and map each to the property of the <Term concept="cia-triad">CIA triad</Term> most at risk, because that property is a strong hint about who would come for it. To make this concrete, take a fictional worked example and carry it through the rest of the post: Northwind Analytics, a mid-size climate-data SaaS company. Three crown jewels stand out. Its proprietary forecasting models are the product's entire competitive edge, so their integrity and confidentiality are what matter. Its customer datasets, holding both PII and the commercial data customers uploaded, are a confidentiality problem. And the availability of the platform itself is a crown jewel, because Northwind's customers cannot work when it is down, which makes downtime someone's leverage.

Notice that the three jewels are not variations on a theme. Each one is attractive to a different adversary, for a different reason, which is exactly why asset-first beats actor-first: the assets tell you which actors to care about.

## Add the Adversaries the Crown Jewels Attract

Now, and only now, bring in the adversaries, mapping each crown jewel to who would want it and why. Do this in terms of archetype and motivation rather than named attribution. You do not need to know that a specific group is behind a threat to reason about it; you need the category and the motive, which is exactly what the Diamond Model's activity-group thinking supports, clustering by how and why rather than by a name on an indictment.

| Crown jewel | CIA property | Who wants it | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forecasting models | Integrity + Confidentiality | Competitor; state economic espionage | Acquire the methodology without paying for the R&D |
| Customer datasets | Confidentiality | Financially motivated criminals; access brokers | Resell PII, extort on commercial data, or sell the access |
| Platform availability | Availability | Ransomware and extortion crews; hacktivists | Downtime is leverage for payment or for a message |
| All three | Any | Insider | Already has access; motivation is grievance or money |

Adversaries align to industry verticals, to specific information types, or to trigger events, and the same asset can draw more than one. ## Target-Centric Analysis: The Model Is the Meeting Place

The method underneath all of this has a name. Robert M. Clark, a former CIA analyst, formalized <Term concept="target-centric-analysis">target-centric analysis</Term> as an alternative to the linear intelligence cycle. In the classic cycle, requirements flow to collectors, collectors hand data to processors, processors hand it to analysts, and analysts hand products to consumers, in a one-directional loop that quietly stovepipes: each stage only talks to its neighbors, and knowledge that should be shared gets siloed.

Target-centric analysis replaces the pipeline with a shared conceptual model of the target that every participant contributes to and draws from, updated continuously as new information arrives and new gaps appear. The threat model you are building is exactly such a shared model. It is not a document one analyst owns and publishes; it is a living picture the whole security function, and ideally the whole business, reasons against together. And every gap the model exposes, every box you cannot fill, is a collection requirement, which is where this connects to collection management: an unanswerable question about an adversary is not a dead end, it is the next thing to go collect.

## Pivot Both Ways

A threat model is not a snapshot; it grows, and it grows by pivoting off two things. Every node you have placed is itself a new target with its own questions.

Pivot off the information, and Northwind's single "customer datasets" node opens up: which datasets exactly, where they physically live, which subset a particular actor would actually want, and what a leak of each would cost. A criminal reselling PII cares about the whole set; a competitor cares only about the commercial data belonging to your biggest shared prospect. Pivot off the adversaries, and the "economic espionage" node opens up the other way: their likely tooling, their operational dependencies, and the trigger events that would turn their attention toward you, such as announcing a new model, winning a marquee contract, or publishing research. Each of these is a fresh node with its own gaps, and this is the moment the model stops being a diagram and becomes a research program, a standing set of questions that generate intelligence requirements as you work them.

## Go As Granular As the Requirement Demands

The obvious next question is how deep to go, and the answer is a dial, not a target. The model should go exactly as granular as a decision needs and no further. You can drill from a system, to a subsystem, to a specific software version, but you stop at the level that answers the question in front of you.

If the requirement is strategic, "which actors should shape our roadmap," you may stop at actor and motivation. If the requirement is feeding vulnerability prioritization, push all the way down, because a critical CVE on an internet-facing subsystem that a crown-jewel-relevant actor is known to exploit should jump the patch queue ahead of a scarier-sounding CVE on a system no relevant actor touches. Granularity is set by the question, not earned by completeness.

## Threat Modeling Is Art, and It Decays

Two honest cautions keep a threat model from being trusted more than it deserves.

It is art, not science. You are assessing likely targeting, and the adversary has a vote. Attackers are human and can act against their own patterns, so the model is a prior that should shape where you look first, not a prophecy that tells you where the attack must come from. The most common way this goes wrong is a specific form of <Term concept="mirror-imaging">mirror imaging</Term> and anchoring: you see a commodity remote-access trojan that public reporting has tied to one named actor, and you conclude that actor is targeting you. But commodity tooling is shared, resold, and reused across many unrelated crews, so the tool tells you very little about the hand holding it. Reading it as attribution, and reshaping your threat model around a group that may have nothing to do with you, is exactly the analytic trap the Analytic Judgment post on this blog is about.

And the model decays. Threats shift, and so does your business. A new product line, an acquisition, a move into a new market, or a single marquee customer can change which crown jewels you hold and which adversaries they attract. A threat model reviewed once and filed is worse than none, because it carries the authority of a current assessment while describing a company that no longer exists. Put it on a revision schedule and tie a review to every significant business change.

## Build It With People Outside Security

Target-centric analysis is built to reduce stovepiping, and that principle has a direct instruction for how you build the model: do it with people outside the security team. Your infosec lens naturally surfaces servers, credentials, and network assets, and it naturally misses things other functions consider obvious. Finance knows the unreleased pricing model is the most sensitive thing in the building. Legal knows which contract terms would be catastrophic if leaked. Product knows the one dataset that is genuinely irreplaceable. Operations knows the single database administrator whose departure would be a crisis. Enumerating crown jewels in a room that includes those people surfaces assets a security-only exercise would never list, and the diversity of contributors is itself a form of collection coverage for the model, the same reason cognitive diversity strengthens analysis generally.

## Hands-On Exercise

Take your own organization, or use Northwind as a sandbox. List three crown jewels and map each to the CIA property most at risk. For each, name the adversary archetype most likely to want it and the motivation in one line, without reaching for a named group. Then pick the highest-value jewel and pivot once off its adversary: write their likely tooling, one operational dependency, and one trigger event that would raise your risk of being targeted. Finally, write the single intelligence requirement this exercise just generated, and the single collection gap it exposed. That pair, a real requirement and a known gap, is what a threat model is for.

## Conclusion

A threat model is how a CTI program earns the right to ignore things. It refuses to start from what is trending and starts instead from what you have, because the assets are what make an adversary care. Built from the crown jewels outward, pivoted both ways, and kept only as granular as your decisions require, it turns the impossible mandate of "watch all threats" into the tractable one of "watch the handful your crown jewels actually attract." That is not a smaller job than watching everything. It is the only version of the job that can actually be done.

For what the model's gaps become once you try to fill them, see the collection management post on this blog. For the requirements this exercise generates, see CTI Foundations Part 1. And for the biases the model has to survive, especially the attribution trap, see Analytic Judgment.
